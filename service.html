Computer Science Tutoring

What began my journey into volunteer work was a sudden opportunity that was presented to me in class. I was a sophomore in high school at the time, taking my third computer science class when my teacher Mr. May told the class that due to family issues, one of the computer science teachers teaching the freshman level computer science course had to take an unexpected leave of absence. Of course, a substitute teacher could easily be found, but it proved to be exceptionally difficult to find one with prior coding experience, which meant that the entire class of students were left to fend for themselves, battling squiggly red lines and compilation errors. Mr. May knew that without guidance, these students would not be able to keep up, especially with a subject like computer science which builds upon itself. Without a solid foundation of knowledge, it can be very hard to progress. When I imagined these poor souls continuing their journeys having to consult stack overflow (ChatGPT has no been invented yet unfortunately), I knew that I had to do something about it.

After being assigned to a timeslot, I began my work and was greeted with a room full of conversation and energy. As you might expect, the idea of having a long-term substitute teacher excited them. You could practically see the evil plots forming in their head on how to take advantage of the change in leadership, but luckily, us tutors were there to intervene. You see, one thing we quickly learned was that as long as you're able to provide students with assignments at a fitting level of difficulty and help with clearing up roadblocks that arise during the software devlopment process, they become genuinely excited to learn the content, ask questions, and try new things. As a tutor, I always sought to balance the task of giving them a good enough hint to help them continue onwards while simultaneosly obscuring the direct answer in order to facilitate the learning process. Throw in a little bit of humor and conversation here and there, and soon it was as if the teacher had never left.

Operation Successful.

Texas Humane Heroes Volunteering

One fateful Winter day, I turned 16. I checked a checkbox to indicate that I was 16 or older, then hit the submit button on my application to become a volunteer at Texas Humane Heroes at precisely 12:03 AM. In six months I wanted to volunteer at the shelter to take some work off the shoulders of the hard workers there, and over time more and more animals ended up needing a bit of help finding a new home.

The first day was a disaster. I was quarantined to a building designated for sick animals, specifically young kittens prone to ringworm infection. A quick trip to google search images instilled a deep sense of nauseous paranoia but I put on my Personal Protective Equipment and got to work, the little guys needed someone to do the job.

The next day I was offered the choice to work at some other station, but I refused. Being a lazy immature kid, I had never worked a day in my life until now. So to make up for it I sacrificed myself for the sake of the other workers and cleaned the kitten rehabilitation ward almost every time I went. There also happened to be a particularly cute kitten who would try to snag my disposable robe everytime I walked by his cage but that's unrelated to my decision.

By the end of my service I emerged with ammonia-singed nostrils and a realization that I was a cat person. They know how to mind their own business and don't suck up to you for attention with little tricks and sometimes like to be thrown onto matresses, running back for another toss (she likes it I swear). Later I came back and adopted two cats that live in my household now, they're pretty chill.

Operation Successful.
